# Ch2 sources — Preparation mechanics

Retrieved: 2026-06-16

- [IAS 7 Statement of Cash Flows — IFRS Foundation](https://www.ifrs.org/issued-standards/list-of-standards/ias-7-statement-of-cash-flows/) — Primary standard; authoritative definitions of all three methods and activity categories.
  - Direct method: "major classes of gross cash receipts and gross cash payments are disclosed"
  - Indirect method: "profit or loss is adjusted for the effects of transactions of a non-cash nature, any deferrals or accruals of past or future operating cash receipts or payments"
  - Operating activities: "the principal revenue-producing activities of the entity and other activities that are not investing or financing activities"
  - Investing activities: "the acquisition and disposal of long-term assets and other investments not included in cash equivalents"
  - Financing activities: "activities that result in changes in the size and composition of the contributed equity and borrowings of the entity"
  - "Investing and financing transactions that do not require the use of cash or cash equivalents are excluded from a statement of cash flows but separately disclosed"

- [Preparation of Direct and Indirect Cash Flows — AnalystPrep CFA Level I](https://analystprep.com/cfa-level-1-exam/financial-reporting-and-analysis/preparation-of-direct-and-indirect-cash-flows/) — CFA-curriculum guide with KTPC Company full numerical example, both methods.
  - Cash from customers = "Revenue less (plus) increase (decrease) in accounts receivable"
  - Cash to suppliers: "Purchases from suppliers = Cost of goods sold less (plus) decrease (increase) in inventory" then "Purchases less (plus) increase (decrease) in accounts payable"
  - KTPC example: Revenue $25,456 − AR increase $39 = cash received $25,417
  - KTPC indirect reconciliation: Net income $3,521 + depreciation $1,100 − gain $220 ± working capital changes = operating cash flow $4,573
  - Equipment sale proceeds = BV $500 + gain $220 = total cash received $720; reported in investing at full amount
  - Dividends paid derived from retained-earnings rollforward: Beginning RE + NI − Ending RE = dividends $2,720

- [Indirect Method — Corporate Finance Institute](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/indirect-method/) — Educational reference with economic rationale for every adjustment type.
  - Non-cash expenses "reduced net income but did not use cash"
  - Deferred taxes are "tax expenses recognised in accounting but not yet paid in cash"
  - Stock-based compensation is "expense related to employee compensation paid in shares and is not a cash outflow"
  - Impairments: "non-cash expense due to reduction in asset value" — added back like depreciation
  - "Unrealised gains and losses do not involve actual cash receipts or payments, so they must be subtracted (for gains) or added back (for losses)"
  - Working capital: current asset increases = "uses of cash"; current liability increases = "sources of cash"

- [Statement of Cash Flows: Preparation — Ryan O'Connell, CFA](https://ryanoconnellfinance.com/statement-of-cash-flows-preparation/) — Full three-section worked example with comparative balance sheet (Meridian Manufacturing Corp.); all numbers verified.
  - "CFO = Net Income + Non-Cash Charges ± Gains/Losses on Disposal ± Changes in Operating Assets and Liabilities"
  - "Report full cash proceeds, not the gain/loss" — equipment sold for $18,000 (BV $10,000 + gain $8,000): investing section shows +$18,000
  - US GAAP classification: interest paid = operating; dividends paid = financing
  - Full CFS: Operating $179,000 − Investing $112,000 − Financing $40,000 = +$27,000 net increase in cash (ties to BS)
  - Dividends paid: Beginning RE $51,000 + NI $162,000 − Ending RE $183,000 = $30,000

- [Cash Flow Statement Indirect Method — Ramp](https://ramp.com/blog/cash-flow-statement-indirect-method) — Practical guide with error taxonomy and sign-rule table.
  - "Forgetting to add back depreciation: The most common error"
  - "Increase in assets … Subtract from net income — cash is tied up"; "Increase in liabilities … Add to net income — cash is retained"
  - "Failing to verify ending cash balance matches the balance sheet" as a distinct checklist item
  - "Misclassifying activities" between operating/investing/financing as a frequent mistake
  - "Double-counting adjustments already in net income"

- [Cash Flow Statement Direct Method — AccountingTools](https://www.accountingtools.com/articles/cash-flow-statement-direct-method) — Concise reference on direct-method structure, advantages, and practical obstacles.
  - Direct method "reveals operating cash receipts and payments"
  - "Information required for the direct method is difficult to assemble; companies simply do not collect and store information in the manner required"
  - Major line items: cash collected from customers, cash paid to employees, cash paid to suppliers, interest paid, income taxes paid
  - Historical restatement becomes "quite difficult" if accounting structure changes

- [Common Mistakes in Cash Flow Statements — Thomson Reuters](https://tax.thomsonreuters.com/blog/common-mistakes-in-cash-flow-statements/) — Practitioner-focused error taxonomy for preparer and reviewer.
  - Investing: "generally related to changes in long-term assets"; financing: "usually related to changes in long-term liabilities and/or equity"
  - Non-cash transaction error: buying equipment with a note payable — "there was actually no cash that changed hands" — wrongly shown as a cash flow
  - "Failure to disclose non-cash transactions need to be corrected"
  - "Accrual balances need to be adjusted to cash basis for proper reporting" of interest and taxes
  - "Negative cash flow, or negative cash from operations" flagged as a red-flag signal

- [Cash Flow: IFRS vs. US GAAP — AnalystPrep CFA Level I](https://analystprep.com/cfa-level-1-exam/financial-reporting-and-analysis/contrast-cash-flow-ifrs-usgaap/) — Authoritative comparison of IAS 7 vs ASC 230 classification choices.
  - Interest paid (IFRS): "may be classified either as an operating activity or financing activity"; US GAAP: operating only
  - Interest received (IFRS): operating or investing; US GAAP: operating only
  - Dividends paid (IFRS): "may be classified either as an operating activity or a financing activity"; US GAAP: financing only
  - Dividends received (IFRS): operating or investing; US GAAP: operating only
  - Companies "must use a consistent classification each year" under IFRS despite flexibility
  - Bank overdrafts: IFRS = cash equivalents; US GAAP = financing activity
