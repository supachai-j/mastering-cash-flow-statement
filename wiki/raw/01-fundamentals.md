# Ch1 sources — Fundamentals & structure

Retrieved: 2026-06-16

---

- [IAS 7 Statement of Cash Flows — IFRS.org](https://www.ifrs.org/issued-standards/list-of-standards/ias-7-statement-of-cash-flows/) — Primary standard-setter source; all core definitions and requirements for IFRS reporters.
  - IAS 7 "prescribes how to present information in a statement of cash flows about how an entity's cash and cash equivalents changed during the period."
  - Objective: "provide users of financial statements with a basis to assess the ability of the entity to generate cash and cash equivalents."
  - **Cash** = "cash on hand and demand deposits."
  - **Cash equivalents** = "short-term, highly liquid investments that are readily convertible to known amounts of cash and that are subject to an insignificant risk of changes in value."
  - **Operating activities** = "the principal revenue-producing activities of the entity and other activities that are not investing or financing activities."
  - **Investing activities** = "acquisition and disposal of long-term assets and other investments not included in cash equivalents."
  - **Financing activities** produce changes in "the size and composition of the contributed equity and borrowings of the entity."
  - Non-cash investing and financing transactions "are excluded from the statement but separately disclosed."

- [Statement of Cash Flows — Corporate Finance Institute](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/statement-of-cash-flows/) — Authoritative pedagogy; structure, line items, direct/indirect method, and inter-statement linkages.
  - "A statement of cash flows is a financial document that shows how cash moves in and out of a business over a period and links the income statement and balance sheet."
  - "Income statement and balance sheet are based on accrual accounting" and don't directly reflect cash activity.
  - Cash equivalents include "bank deposits, short-term investments, and any very easily cash-convertible assets" with maturities under three months.
  - Operating CF: net income (indirect starting point) + D&A + working capital adjustments (A/R, inventory, A/P).
  - Investing CF: "acquisition and disposal of non-current assets and other investments" — CapEx, securities, proceeds from asset sales.
  - Financing CF: "changes in a company's capital structure" — debt issuance/repayment, equity issuance, buybacks, dividends.
  - "The closing cash balance from the statement feeds directly into the balance sheet's current assets section."
  - "Both direct and indirect methods yield identical operating cash flow totals; only presentation differs."

- [Indirect Method — Corporate Finance Institute](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/accounting/indirect-method/) — Precise step-by-step explanation of the indirect method with a worked numerical example.
  - Indirect method is "a standard accounting technique to reconcile net income (from the income statement) to the cash provided by or used in operating activities."
  - Steps: (1) start with net income; (2) add back non-cash expenses (D&A, SBC); (3) adjust for non-operating items; (4) account for working capital changes.
  - Worked example: Net income $10M + D&A $2.5M − ΔAR $3M − ΔInventory $1.5M + ΔAP $2.2M − ΔOther CL $0.7M = **CFO $9.5M**.
  - Indirect "dominates practice because the data is readily available from a company's standard accrual-based income statement and balance sheet."
  - Direct method "lists major classes of gross cash receipts and payments" but "requires tracking and aggregating every single cash transaction."

- [Statement of Cash Flows Under ASC 230 — BDO](https://arch.bdo.com/Statement-of-Cash-Flows-Under-ASC-230) — Authoritative Big-4 US GAAP (ASC 230) guide; classification rules, direct/indirect, restricted cash.
  - ASC 230 requires a CFS showing "the change during the period in the total of cash, cash equivalents, restricted cash and restricted cash equivalents."
  - "ASC 230 is largely principles-based," requiring "significant judgment" in classification.
  - ASC 230-10-45-25 "encourages" the direct method; most entities use indirect.
  - Indirect method "adjusts net income for revenue and expense items that were not the result of operating cash transactions."
  - Gross presentation generally required; netting permitted when "turnover is quick, amounts are large, and maturities are short."
  - Transfers between cash, cash equivalents, restricted cash "don't constitute cash flows since no external transaction occurs."
  - Multi-characteristic transactions classified "based on the predominant source or use of cash flows."

- [Cash Flow Statement — Wall Street Prep](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/cash-flow-statement/) — Investment-banking-level pedagogy; accrual/cash distinction, three-section examples, articulation.
  - CFS "reconciles net income with actual cash movements, tracking real inflows and outflows across three distinct activities."
  - "Accrual-basis profits are easier to manipulate than cash-basis profits" — a company can show strong earnings while burning cash.
  - Direct method "explicitly lists cash receipts and payments to third parties, bypassing net income as the starting point."
  - Worked numbers: CFO $48M (after +$10M D&A and −$20M working capital increase), CFI −$40M (CapEx), CFF −$5M (debt amortization).
  - "Net income flows from the income statement into CFO, working capital changes derive from balance sheet comparisons, and the ending cash balance calculated on the CFS becomes the balance sheet's cash account figure."

- [How Are the Three Financial Statements Linked? — Wall Street Prep](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/how-are-the-financial-statements-linked/) — Definitive pedagogy on articulation between CFS, income statement, and balance sheet.
  - "The starting line item of the cash flow statement (CFS) is net income from the income statement under the indirect approach."
  - "CFO = Net Income + D&A – Increase in Change in Working Capital."
  - Increase in operating current assets = cash outflow; decrease = cash inflow; increase in operating current liabilities = cash inflow.
  - "Ending PP&E = Beginning PP&E + Capital Expenditures (Capex) – Depreciation" — IS and BS feed the investing section.
  - "The ending cash balance at the bottom of the cash flow statement flows to the balance sheet as the cash balance for the current period."
  - Numerical example: Beginning cash $60M + net change $50M = Ending cash $110M (becomes beginning cash next period).

- [Link Between CFS, Income Statement, and Balance Sheet — AnalystPrep (CFA Level 1)](https://analystprep.com/cfa-level-1-exam/financial-reporting-and-analysis/link-between-cash-flow-statement-and-income-and-balance-sheet-statement/) — CFA curriculum-aligned articulation mechanics.
  - "Beginning cash balance + Cash inflows − Cash outflows (across operating, investing, financing) = Ending cash balance."
  - Retained earnings bridge: "Beginning RE + Net Income – Dividends = Ending RE" — shows how IS → CFS → BS equity connects.
  - "When accrual revenues exceed cash receipts, accounts receivable increases" — articulation between IS revenue and CFS/BS.
  - Working capital equation: "Beginning A/R + Revenue – Cash collected from customers = Ending A/R" — links IS to BS asset changes.
  - "Accrual accounting means reported figures may not directly correspond to cash transactions," requiring adjustments.

- [Cash Flow Statements: Common Myths and Mistakes — BDO UK](https://www.bdo.co.uk/en-gb/insights/audit-and-assurance/cash-flow-statements-common-myths-and-mistakes) — Big-4 practitioner guide to classification errors under IFRS.
  - Myth: inter-company balances always operating. Reality: parent-to-subsidiary funding flows should be investing or financing.
  - Myth: all PPE acquisition/disposal = investing. Reality: property held for rental then sold as inventory = operating.
  - Myth: all interest payments = financing. Reality: IFRS allows interest paid as operating or financing.
  - Myth: all lease payments = financing. Reality: variable, short-term, low-value lease payments classify differently.
  - "Must include demand overdrafts as cash equivalents in cash flow statements, though presented as liabilities on balance sheets."
  - "Cash from factoring should be financing activities, not operating (it's a collateralized loan)."

- [Common Cash Equivalents Trap — ACCA AB Magazine](https://abmagazine.accaglobal.com/global/articles/2021/apr/technical/common-cash-equivalents-trap.html) — Professional body analysis of the most frequent cash equivalents misclassification errors.
  - Three-month rule applies "from the acquisition date," not from the reporting period end — common confusion.
  - Banker's acceptances with 6-month original maturity don't qualify even if 3 months remain.
  - "BAs with a larger discount would mean that there is an increased risk of changes in value" — fails insignificant-risk test.
  - Equity shares fail because investors "bear the significant risk of changes in share price."
  - All three criteria must be met simultaneously: short-term + highly liquid + insignificant risk of value change.
  - Companies must "disclose their judgment policy" on what they classify as cash equivalents (IAS 7.48).

- [Definition of Cash and Cash Equivalents (ASC 230) — Deloitte DART](https://dart.deloitte.com/USDART/home/codification/presentation/asc230-10/roadmap-statement-cash-flow/chapter-4-cash-cash-equivalents/4-1-definition-cash-cash-equivalents) — Deloitte's authoritative reading of the ASC 230 definition.
  - Cash = "currency on hand but demand deposits with banks or other financial institutions" allowing unrestricted deposits/withdrawals.
  - Cash equivalents must be "readily convertible to known amounts of cash" AND "so near maturity that they present insignificant risk of changes in value because of changes in interest rates."
  - "Original maturities of three months or less" — measured from the entity's *acquisition date*, not from the balance sheet date.
  - A Treasury note bought three years ago doesn't become a cash equivalent when 3 months remain — unless it was acquired with 3 months left to run.
  - Qualifying examples: Treasury bills, commercial paper, money market funds, federal funds sold, bank repos under 3 months.
  - Entities establish their own accounting policies on which qualifying investments to treat as cash equivalents (policy election).

- [Cash Flow Statement — Financial Edge (fe.training)](https://www.fe.training/free-resources/accounting/cash-flow-statement/) — Professional training provider summary; preparation steps and BS reconciliation mechanism.
  - CFS "presents the cash flows for the designated period and it reconciles to the cash and cash equivalents number on the balance sheet."
  - Indirect method (most common) reconciles net income by adjusting for non-cash items and working capital changes.
  - A healthy company should show "consistent positive cash flow from operations" — a key signal of sustainability beyond accounting profits.
  - Preparation steps: calculate BS changes, perform BASE reconciliations, summarize working capital lines, reconcile to ending cash.

- [Relationships Between Key Financial Statements — Lumen Learning](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/wm-accountingformanagers/chapter/relationships-between-key-financial-statements/) — University-level textbook on articulation mechanics.
  - CFS "reconciles beginning cash and cash equivalents from the balance sheet (ending cash from the prior set of financial statements) to ending cash from the current balance sheet."
  - Flow chain: Net income → Statement of Owners' Equity → Balance Sheet equity; Net income → CFS operating section.
  - Primary function: "reconciling accrual basis accounting to cash basis."
