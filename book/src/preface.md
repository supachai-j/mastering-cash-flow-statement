# Preface & How to Use This Book

Most people learn to read the income statement first. They learn what revenue is, what net profit is, and they stop there — treating the balance sheet as an afterthought and the cash flow statement as the dense, unloved third page nobody reads. This book exists because that ordering is exactly backwards for anyone who wants to understand whether a business is actually healthy.

The cash flow statement is the lie detector of financial reporting. Revenue can be booked before a customer pays. Profit can be shaped with depreciation policies, capitalized costs, and one-off gains. But the line that says *"cash and cash equivalents at end of period"* has to agree with what is actually in the bank. That hard constraint is what makes the statement of cash flows the analyst's most trustworthy instrument — and the subject of this guide.

## Who this book is for

- **Students** preparing for accounting, finance, CFA, or MBA coursework who want the cash flow statement to finally *click*.
- **Analysts and investors** who want to move past "net income" and judge a company on the cash it truly generates.
- **Founders, operators, and finance staff** who need to forecast cash, manage liquidity, and survive the gap between profit and payday.
- **Auditors and the financially curious** who want to spot when the numbers are being dressed up.

You need only a basic grasp of the income statement and balance sheet. Everything else is built up from first principles.

### Notation & conventions

- **Cash inflows are positive; outflows are negative** (shown in parentheses in formal statements, e.g. `(1,200)`).
- **Δ** ("delta") means *change in* — e.g. `ΔAR` is the change in accounts receivable over the period.
- **OCF / CFO** = cash flow from operating activities. **CFI** = investing. **CFF** = financing.
- Formulas are written in plain notation (no special math fonts) so they read the same on screen and in print, e.g. `FCF = OCF − CapEx`.
- Standards are cited inline as `IAS 7` (IFRS) and `ASC 230` (US GAAP), with paragraph references where precision matters.
- Currency is shown as generic units (`$`) unless a real case requires otherwise; the mechanics are currency-agnostic.
