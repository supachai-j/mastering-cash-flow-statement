## Quiz 1 — Decoding the Cash Flow Statement

**Q1. Why is the cash flow statement so important for most businesses?**
a) It shows true cash flow by cutting out non-cash expenses
b) It lists all the company's debts
c) It tracks employee salaries only
d) It predicts future stock prices

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> It strips non-cash items (depreciation, stock-based comp, unrealized gains) out of net income to reveal how much real cash the business produced. <em>(Chapter 1)</em></p>
</details>

**Q2. What does positive operating cash flow tell you?**
a) The business's earnings are positive
b) Its operations are producing real cash
c) It's spending more than it earns
d) It's avoiding all investments

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> Positive operating cash flow means the core business is generating cash — not just accounting profit. <em>(Chapter 3)</em></p>
</details>

**Q3. Why is it a red flag if cash from investing exceeds operating cash flow (as it has at Intel)?**
a) Operations are losing money
b) It's investing more than it earns, so it needs outside funding (debt or dilution)
c) It's cutting back on spending
d) It's paying off all its debts

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> If investing outflows are bigger than the cash operations produce, the gap must be filled by borrowing or issuing shares — a sign free cash flow is negative. <em>(Chapter 8)</em></p>
</details>

**Q4. What figure does the cash flow statement (indirect method) start with?**
a) Operating cash flow
b) Revenue
c) Zero
d) Net income

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">d)</span> The indirect method begins with net income, then adds back non-cash items and adjusts for working-capital changes. <em>(Chapters 1–2)</em></p>
</details>

**Q5. Why is low or no stock-based compensation a green flag?**
a) It boosts dilution
b) It avoids diluting shareholders
c) It increases insider selling
d) It cuts operating cash flow

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> Stock-based comp is a real cost to owners in the form of dilution; little or none keeps your ownership stake intact. <em>(Chapter 8)</em></p>
</details>

**Q6. What's the problem with high stock-based compensation (as at Snap)?**
a) It aligns insiders with shareholders perfectly
b) It lowers operating cash flow
c) It saves the business cash
d) It dilutes shareholders and can misalign insider and shareholder incentives

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">d)</span> Heavy SBC dilutes existing owners and can let insiders get rich regardless of how the stock performs. <em>(Chapter 8)</em></p>
</details>

**Q7. Why is it good when CapEx is much less than operating cash flow (as at Adobe)?**
a) It means negative free cash flow
b) It keeps profits low
c) It means strong positive free cash flow
d) It stops all growth

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">c)</span> Free cash flow = operating cash flow − CapEx, so low CapEx leaves a large, growing FCF for owners. <em>(Chapters 3 & 8)</em></p>
</details>

**Q8. What's a red flag with a dividend (as with BCE's payout)?**
a) It's growing steadily
b) It's well covered by free cash flow
c) It's larger than the free cash flow the business generates
d) It was cut to save cash

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">c)</span> A dividend bigger than FCF must be funded by debt or dilution — a sign it may be unsustainable. <em>(Chapter 8)</em></p>
</details>

**Q9. Why is growing free cash flow a big win?**
a) More cash for dividends, buybacks, or reinvestment
b) The business is losing market share
c) Profits are declining
d) Cash is tied up in debt

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> Growing FCF is growing real profit — fuel for shareholder returns and reinvestment. <em>(Chapters 3 & 8)</em></p>
</details>

**Q10. What does persistent negative free cash flow (as at Walgreens) usually warn of?**
a) The business is thriving
b) It may be struggling with profits, competition, or debt costs
c) It's saving cash for the future
d) It's repaying all its debt

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> Sustained negative FCF with no offsetting growth story points to weakening profitability or rising debt service. <em>(Chapters 5 & 8)</em></p>
</details>

## Quiz 2 — Using the Three Statements Together

**Q1. Why look at all three financial statements together?**
a) You get a fuller picture of the business's finances
b) Only to track cash
c) To predict the stock this year
d) To list salaries

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> Income statement (profitability) + balance sheet (financial position) + cash flow statement (cash reality) together tell the whole story. <em>(Introduction & Chapter 1)</em></p>
</details>

**Q2. What's a strong sign on a company's income statement (as at Google)?**
a) Revenue growing slower than the share count
b) Net income and earnings per share growing
c) Shares outstanding growing
d) Nothing — the income statement doesn't matter

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> Rising net income *and* EPS means profit is growing on a per-share basis — value creation for owners.</p>
</details>

**Q3. What does it tell you when current assets exceed total liabilities (as at Google)?**
a) The business is drowning in debt
b) It could pay off all its debts and is financially solid
c) Its cash is gone
d) Liabilities outpace assets

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> More current assets than *total* liabilities is a sign of exceptional balance-sheet strength.</p>
</details>

**Q4. Which items live in the financing section of the cash flow statement?**
a) Dividends, buybacks, debt repayments
b) Cash, revenue, shares outstanding
c) Revenue, gross profit, operating income
d) Cash, total liabilities, total assets

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> Financing activities show money raised or returned: debt, dilution, buybacks, and dividends. <em>(Chapter 1)</em></p>
</details>

**Q5. Why is a falling cash balance plus heavy debt (as at Beyond Meat) a worry?**
a) It isn't growing
b) It's stable and strong
c) Cash exceeds liabilities
d) It's burning cash while carrying a large debt load it must eventually repay

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">d)</span> Shrinking cash + large debt = a looming question of how the debt gets repaid. Combine the balance sheet (debt, cash) with the cash flow statement (burn) to see it. <em>(Chapter 8)</em></p>
</details>

**Q6. What makes a company like Beyond Meat a risky investment?**
a) Flat revenue
b) Losing significant money, weak gross margins, and running low on cash
c) It's cutting debt quickly
d) It's boosting free cash flow

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> A business that can't sell its product profitably and is running out of cash has limited options — high bankruptcy risk.</p>
</details>

**Q7. What makes an income statement look excellent (as at Visa)?**
a) Revenue and profit rising while the share count shrinks
b) Net income crashing
c) Revenue flat for years
d) Shares outstanding doubling

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> Growing profit on a *shrinking* share base supercharges per-share value — the buyback flywheel.</p>
</details>

**Q8. Which metrics are found on the income statement?**
a) Revenue, liabilities, cash flow
b) Cash, liabilities, assets
c) Revenue, gross profit, net income
d) Operating cash flow, shares outstanding, debt

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">c)</span> Revenue, gross profit, operating income, and net income all live on the income statement.</p>
</details>

**Q9. What are the three sections of the cash flow statement?**
a) Operating, investing, financing
b) Income, debt, free cash flow
c) Cash, liabilities, assets
d) Dividends, buybacks, debt

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> Operating, Investing, and Financing — the backbone of the whole book. <em>(Chapter 1)</em></p>
</details>

**Q10. What's a standout feature of Constellation Software's cash flow statement?**
a) High stock-based compensation
b) Tiny CapEx relative to large operating cash flow
c) Negative free cash flow
d) Heavy debt reliance

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> Very low CapEx against large operating cash flow means almost all of its cash becomes free cash flow for owners. <em>(Chapter 8)</em></p>
</details>

## Quiz 3 — Best Valuation Multiple, and When

**Q1. What does a price multiple like P/E tell you about a stock?**
a) How many years of earnings you're paying for
b) The price relative to its earnings
c) How "expensive" the stock is
d) All of the above

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">d)</span> A price multiple is all three at once — it's the price tag relative to profit. <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q2. Why might a P/E of 50 mean a stock is expensive?**
a) It's losing money
b) You're paying 50× each dollar of earnings — ~50 years to earn your money back at today's profit
c) Earnings aren't growing
d) Its P/E is above its history

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> A high multiple is only justified if profits grow fast enough to shrink that payback period. <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q3. When is the P/E ratio best used?**
a) Banks, insurance, and other financials
b) Growth companies with big CapEx
c) Businesses with no profits
d) Real estate

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> For financials, earnings are the steadiest profit measure; cash-flow multiples are distorted by deposits and loans. <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q4. Why isn't P/FCF useful for a bank like JPMorgan?**
a) Its cash flows swing wildly with deposits and loans
b) It's too stable to judge
c) Banks don't report cash flows
d) It matches earnings perfectly

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> Lending and deposit flows make a bank's reported cash flow lurch by huge amounts — its P/FCF is meaningless. <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q5. Why does P/FCF fit Constellation Software well?**
a) It's a bank
b) It has huge growth CapEx
c) Its net income is depressed by non-cash amortization, while FCF is steady and representative
d) Its market cap ignores cash flow

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">c)</span> Heavy acquisition amortization (non-cash) understates net income, so cash flow better reflects true profit. <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q6. When should you reach for P/OCF (as with Amazon)?**
a) Flat-cash-flow businesses
b) Growth companies investing heavily in CapEx (which depresses FCF)
c) Lenders with stable loans
d) Firms with no expenses

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> When growth CapEx pushes FCF near zero, operating cash flow shows the earning power *before* growth spending — but check that the spending is actually paying off. <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q7. Why might P/OI suit a real-estate firm whose net income jumps around?**
a) It's less consistent than earnings
b) It has no real estate
c) Net income swings on unrealized property revaluations, but operating income is smooth
d) It ignores all expenses

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">c)</span> Operating income strips out volatile fair-value gains/losses, giving a steadier valuation anchor. <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q8. What's the appeal of P/EBITDA for a high-depreciation utility?**
a) It's perfect for low-CapEx firms
b) It's less stable than net income
c) It tracks revenue only
d) It's steadier than net income because it adds back heavy non-cash depreciation

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">d)</span> But beware — EBITDA ignores the very real CapEx those depreciation charges represent, so it overstates "profit." <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q9. Why avoid the P/S ratio for an unprofitable company?**
a) It shows profit trends clearly
b) It says nothing about profitability — the thing that ultimately matters
c) It's best for unprofitable firms
d) It matches cash flow

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">b)</span> Revenue without profit is hollow; valuing on sales alone can be deeply misleading. <em>(Chapter 9)</em></p>
</details>

**Q10. What's the key tip when a company reports its own metric (e.g. "adjusted EBITDA", FFO, CAFD)?**
a) Check the filing to see exactly what's added back and removed, then judge if it's honest
b) Use it without checking the adjustments
c) Apply it to every business
d) Ignore it entirely

<details class="quiz"><summary>Show answer</summary>
<p><span class="ans">a)</span> Self-defined "profit" can quietly strip out real costs — verify the bridge before you trust the number. <em>(Chapters 5 & 9)</em></p>
</details>
