# Glossary

Key terms used throughout the book. Standard names (IAS 7, ASC 230) and abbreviations (FCFF) are
kept in their conventional English form.

**Accruals.** The non-cash portion of earnings — the difference between net income and cash flow. High accruals relative to cash flow signal lower earnings quality (Chapter 5).

**Accrual accounting.** Recognizing revenue when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of when cash moves. The basis of the income statement and the reason a cash flow statement is needed.

**Amortization.** The systematic expensing of an intangible asset's cost over its useful life. A non-cash item added back in the indirect method.

**Cash and cash equivalents.** Cash on hand plus short-term, highly liquid investments readily convertible to known amounts of cash with insignificant risk of value change (original maturity ≤ 3 months). The figure the statement reconciles to.

**Cash conversion cycle (CCC).** DSO + DIO − DPO — the number of days cash is tied up in operations before it returns. Lower is better for cash flow.

**CapEx (capital expenditure).** Cash spent to acquire or upgrade long-lived assets (PP&E). The main outflow in the investing section and the bridge from CFO to free cash flow.

**CFO / CFI / CFF.** Cash flow from Operating / Investing / Financing activities — the three sections of the statement.

**Direct method.** Presenting operating cash flow as gross cash receipts and payments (cash from customers, cash to suppliers, etc.). More transparent; less common in practice.

**Discounted cash flow (DCF).** A valuation that projects future free cash flows and discounts them to present value (Chapter 7).

**Free cash flow (FCF).** Cash from operations after the investment needed to maintain the business; broadly `CFO − CapEx`. See **FCFF** and **FCFE**.

**FCFF (free cash flow to firm).** Cash available to *all* capital providers (debt and equity), before financing. Discounted at **WACC** to give enterprise value.

**FCFE (free cash flow to equity).** Cash available to *equity holders* after debt payments and net borrowing. Discounted at the **cost of equity** to give equity value.

**Indirect method.** Presenting operating cash flow by starting from net income and adjusting for non-cash items and changes in working capital. The most common method.

**IAS 7.** The IFRS standard *Statement of Cash Flows* (Chapter 4).

**ASC 230.** The US GAAP topic *Statement of Cash Flows* (Chapter 4).

**Net working capital (NWC).** Current operating assets minus current operating liabilities (excluding cash and debt). Changes in NWC adjust net income to cash in the indirect method.

**Non-cash items.** Expenses or gains with no cash effect (depreciation, amortization, impairments, stock-based compensation, deferred taxes) — added back or reversed in the indirect method.

**Operating cash flow (OCF).** Cash generated by the core business; the single most important line on the statement.

**Quality of earnings.** The degree to which reported profit is backed by cash. Measured by comparing CFO to net income (Chapter 3 & 5).

**Terminal value (TV).** The estimated value of all cash flows beyond the explicit forecast horizon in a DCF, via perpetuity growth or an exit multiple.

**WACC (weighted-average cost of capital).** The blended required return of debt and equity, used to discount FCFF.

**Window dressing.** Timing or classifying transactions near period-end to flatter the statement (e.g. delaying supplier payments to boost CFO). A red flag (Chapter 5).

**Working-capital sign rule.** Asset increases consume cash (−); liability increases provide cash (+). The core mechanic of the indirect method.
